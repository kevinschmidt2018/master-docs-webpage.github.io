As the city makes plans for a new lakefront landscape, residents remind developers that they're not over the pedestrian underpass headache

By Kevin Schmidt
Medill - Northwestern University

North Lake Shore Drive at Belmont Harbor

North Lake Shore Drive at Belmont Harbor

Manhattan is surrounded by water on nearly every side, but no resident willingly goes for a dip in the East River. Los Angeles is a quintessential Californian metropolis, but the downtown area lies miles from the edge of the Pacific. In Chicago, urban hustle meets beachfront quiescence, and there’s no place to better see this contrast than in the city’s lakefront parks.

Driving along Lake Shore Drive just north of Navy Pier, the waterfront parkway straddles and even cuts through the lakefront park, offering a stimulating driving experience. But it’s the pedestrian that pays, faced with limited means to access the park on foot or bike, most likely through an underpass. But unflattering descriptions of these tunnels are spurring Illinois Department of Transportation to reconsider pedestrian access as the agency redevelops the parkway as a whole.

“The viaducts and underpasses aren’t safe,” said Jenna Brose, a 29-year-old resident of River North. “They’re dark. As I woman, I wouldn’t feel safe there.”

Brose and other Chicago residents expressed their concerns on how North Lake Shore Drive can better accommodate pedestrians during a public meeting last month. The meeting was held as on open forum that allowed attendees to study alternatives for the stretch of the lakefront parkway from Grand Avenue to Hollywood Avenue and discuss the proposals with project team members.

Safety in and around underpasses was brought to light when 25-year-old Raven Lemons was shot to death in Streeterville while standing near the pedestrian tunnel to Ohio Street Beach. The incident prompted city officials to close the underpass nightly from midnight to 5 a.m. until October.

Edgewater resident Susan McClain was critical of the current state of some viaducts near her neighborhood. “Wilson and Lawrence are both really decrepit,” she said. “A bridge is safer, and it’s a better view. If it’s late at night, you would never go under a bridge unless someone was around.”

David Kostelansky, who lives near the underpass at Chicago Avenue, expressed his issues with the access point.

“We get a steady stream of people until late at night coming down Chicago Avenue and going underneath,” said the 66-year-old resident, who’s also president of the Lake Shore Park Advisory Council. “I’ve stood there with an off-duty armed policeman in the park watching drug transactions going on.”

In hopes of addressing and answering these questions, crime data from July 2014 through July 2017 was obtained by the Chicago Data Portal, narrowing in on the area that is defined as north of Grand Avenue, east of Lake Shore Drive, south of Hollywood Avenue and, naturally, west of the Lake Michigan shoreline. The data revolves around five crimes: robbery, battery, assault, sex offenses and homicide.

Key points of the findings are summarized as such:

  • The data revealed 577 violent crimes reported in the three-year period from July 2014 to July 2017. Battery accounted for the highest number of crimes at 373. There were 99 reports of assault, 84 reports of robbery, 18 sex offenses, and three homicides in the studied area. 131 of the reported crimes ended up as an arrest.
  • Saturday and Sundays saw the highest concentration of crime, with 117 and 106 reported, respectively. Monday was the highest day of the work week for crime, with battery being the highest offense.
  • The highest number of crimes were committed on park property, with 181 of the 577 taking place; 145 crimes were committed on the street, and 120 on a sidewalk.
  • The highest number of crimes were committed at 4 p.m., 8 p.m. and 9 p.m., with 42 reports each during those hours. The safest hour (the hour with the fewest number of reported crimes) was 5 a.m.

The Results

Review of the lakefront crime data neither confirms nor denies the concerns from residents that the underpasses are unsafe, but it does give a glimpse into where and when higher concentrations of crimes are committed in the defined area.

While the public perception may be negative, this perception may also be influenced by other factors: poor lighting, crumbing facades, proximity to a busy street, noise level, etc. These, along with previously expressed concerns, are incentives for the city and the state to obtain specific feedback from the public on what improvements would be most effective.

Hosted by the Illinois Department of Transportation and the Chicago Department of Transportation, open houses allow project team members from IDOT to counter previously expressed concerns with a variety of proposals for attendees to consider.

“Do you want an underpass? The ones that we would propose would be like the ones on [Museum Campus] with the grand, wide, lighted underpasses,” said Kimberly Murphy, project manager with the Illinois Department of Transportation. “Or do you want an overpass? Or do you want to just cross the street at grade?”

The underpass near Roosevelt Road provides pedestrian access to the lakefront and museums.

The Roosevelt Road underpass near Museum Campus was redesigned in 1998.

An audio-visual presentation and imaged renderings were on display to “get people to start reimaging how the access would be, because they’re not so great,” said Murphy. “Some places don’t have ramps, you have small stairs, there’s dripping water, and they’re not inviting. We’re looking to change that by making it easy to find, something that’s inviting, something that connects the lakefront to the west side of Lake Shore Drive.”

Chicago residents review redevelopment proposals at a North Lake Shore Drive public meeting.

“The idea in some of these drawings of having on overpass which could be lit is so much smarter because it could be visible and you don’t have to worry about the people hiding in there at night,” said Kostelansky. “I like the idea, and the community likes the idea.”
